𝗞𝗹𝗼𝘁𝘀𝗸𝗶

  • It’s a sliding block puzzle that originated in China, themed after the historical escape of General Cao Cao during the Three Kingdoms period.
  • You have a grid with blocks of different shapes and sizes.
  • The goal: move the largest block (representing Cao Cao) through the exit at the bottom of the board.
  • Rules: you can only slide one block at a time, and blocks can’t overlap or leave the grid.
  • It’s a game of spatial reasoning and planning ahead.

𝟮𝟬𝟰𝟴

  • A single-player sliding tile puzzle game.
  • The board is a 4×4 grid where numbered tiles appear (usually 2 or 4).
  • Each move slides all tiles in one direction (up, down, left, right).
  • When two tiles with the same number collide, they merge into a new tile with their sum (e.g., 2+2=4, 4+4=8).
  • The goal: combine numbers until you create the tile 2048 (and beyond, if you keep going).
  • It’s simple in mechanics but surprisingly deep in strategy.

𝗟𝗮𝗯𝘆𝗿𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗵

  • A maze is essentially a puzzle of pathways.
  • You usually start at an entrance and must find your way to an exit, avoiding dead ends.
  • Mazes can be drawn on paper, built physically (like hedge mazes), or generated digitally in games.
  • Algorithms like depth-first search, breadth-first search, or A* pathfinding are commonly used to solve mazes computationally.
  • It’s about navigation and problem-solving under uncertainty.

𝗠𝗶𝗻𝗲𝘀𝘄𝗲𝗲𝗽𝗶𝗻𝗴

  • A classic logic puzzle that comes with many operating systems (famously Windows).
  • The game is played on a grid of covered cells. Some cells hide mines.
  • Clicking on a cell reveals either:
    1. A mine (game over)
    2. A number showing how many adjacent cells contain mines.
  • Using logic and deduction, you must flag the locations of all mines without triggering them.
  • It blends probability, risk-taking, and careful reasoning.
